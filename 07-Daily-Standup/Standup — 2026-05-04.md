## Key Insights

### Henry Ford / electricity analogy (Wolf)
The way factories had to physically restructure their layouts to take advantage of electricity — the same fundamental restructuring might be needed for SMEs to take advantage of AI. The efficiency gain from AI isn't just "plug in a tool" — it requires rethinking how the business is built and operated. If we figure out how to do that for SMEs, that's the real prize. Filed as a big-picture framing, not an immediate action.

### "AI-native queryable company" concept (Giuseppe)
From a YC video: companies being built as entirely "queryable" — every interaction, decision, and process is captured and searchable. Our own vault + recording process is an experiment in this. If we build our startup this way from day zero, we become the proof of concept for what we're selling.

### Process mining vs knowledge mining (joint)
Celonis plugs into ERP and mines processes. We would plug into people (interviews, shadowing, computer-use tracking) and mine knowledge — specifically HOW and WHY decisions are made, not just WHAT happens. For companies with ERPs (like Linka), we could also plug into the ERP, but the differentiator is the human knowledge layer above it.

### ICP might need to move upward (joint)
The Italian interviews (real estate, glass factory) were too small to be commercially interesting as standalone customers. John Lynch (€40-50M, 250 employees) is far enough along in digitalization that plugging in AI is easy. The sweet spot might be between — companies big enough to pay but not so big that enterprise tools already serve them. M&A angle changes this: if we prepare companies for sale, even the small ones become viable because the fee is a % of the deal.
