### The ERP Story

This is the most concrete data point in the interview:

| ERP                  | Approximate cost                  |
| -------------------- | --------------------------------- |
| 1st system           | €10,000                           |
| 2nd system           | €50,000                           |
| 3rd system           | Unknown                           |
| 4th system (current) | €1M+ (possibly multiple millions) |

Every ERP implementation was "incredibly resource-intensive, staff-intensive, everything." They are on their FOURTH attempt at systematizing their operations — and each time it costs an order of magnitude more.

**Implication:** This is a company that demonstrably invests in operational infrastructure. They are not allergic to spending money on systems. But ERP is a blunt instrument — it systematizes transactions, not judgment. The knowledge trapped in people's heads survives every ERP implementation unchanged.

## What We Heard

### His overall reaction

- Found the subject **"very interesting"**
- Felt the company is **"under-advised"** on AI — they simply haven't addressed it because they're busy with other things (like the ERP implementation)
- Key quote sentiment: **"The big AI players don't really consider us because we're not where the big money is"**

### What this tells us

1. **Willingness to engage exists.** He didn't dismiss the conversation — he leaned in and agreed to a follow-up.
2. **The "under-advised" framing is gold.** This is how the SME owner FEELS — not "I have a knowledge problem" but "I feel like everyone's talking about AI and nobody's talking to ME." Our product could be framed as: "We're the AI advisor that actually comes to companies your size."
3. **The "big players ignore us" gap is real and felt.** This validates exactly what [[Interview 003 - Merih (Finance Professor)]] surfaced (via Clonable) and what [[Interview 002 - Ex-Banker, BoD of Deutsche Bank, SMEs view]] flagged about enterprise tools not scaling down. John is experiencing the gap firsthand.
4. **He's in the middle of a massive systems project.** The ERP implementation means (a) he's thinking about operational infrastructure, which is our territory, (b) he has budget for it, but (c) he's resource-constrained right now and might not have bandwidth for another project.

## Patterns This Confirms or Challenges

### Confirms

- **[[Patterns From Fieldwork]] Pattern 1 — Profitability masks fragility:** Linka is successful (€40–50M), investing in systems, but hasn't touched AI or knowledge management. No urgency.
- **[[Patterns From Fieldwork]] Pattern 8 — Parallel delivery beats sequential:** He's deep in a multi-million ERP implementation. Adding another "planning-only" project would be a non-starter. Any engagement with a company like this needs a tangible quick win.
- **The "big players ignore mid-market" gap** — validated firsthand by the CEO experiencing it.

### Challenges

- **ICP size assumption:** We said €2–20M. This company is €40–50M and has the same "under-advised on AI" problem. Either our wedge is too narrow, or the problem is universal across all SME sizes but the solution differs.
- **"No formal processes" assumption:** A company on its 4th ERP system HAS formal processes for transactions. The trapped knowledge is in the decision-making layer ABOVE the ERP — the judgment calls, the customer relationship nuances, the "why we do it this way" that no ERP captures.

### New observation

- **The ERP ceiling:** ERP systematizes what's already understood. It doesn't capture what's tacit. A company can spend millions on ERP and still have all its critical knowledge in the founder's head. This is a potentially powerful positioning angle: "ERP captures your processes. We capture your expertise."

## Questions for the Follow-up

Beyond the 4 we already sent, probe these specifically:

1. **"Your ERP captures your transactions. What does it NOT capture?"** — this should surface the tacit knowledge layer
2. **"If your most experienced employee left tomorrow, what would break?"** — tests the knowledge-dependency pattern
3. **"You mentioned feeling under-advised on AI. What would a useful AI conversation look like for a company like yours?"** — lets him design the product for us
4. **"Have you thought about what happens to this business in 5–10 years? Not selling, just... what comes after you?"** — opens the succession door gently
5. **"Who in your company knows things that nobody else knows?"** — identifies the knowledge holders
