## What We Learned

### On knowledge and processes
- **No process documentation exists.** "Tracciare il processo, no." Transactions are tracked through the gestionale. Decision-making, product knowledge, customer advice — none of it is documented.
- **65,000 articles require deep product knowledge.** Even knowing which product to recommend for which situation takes years. The knowledge is relational (product ↔ use case ↔ customer need), not just transactional.
- **The business is shifting from products to advice.** Luigi explicitly says they sell "consiglio" (advice) more than products. The value proposition IS the tacit knowledge — knowing what the artisan actually needs. This is the exact expertise our product would capture.
- **Gestionale handles transactions only.** "Easy" manages sales, barcodes, invoices — but "i processi interni ce li diamo noi" (internal processes are ours to manage). The gestionale ceiling confirmed again.
- **Knowledge transfer happens by proximity and memory.** A colleague tells the new person "a year ago we did this." No database of exceptions. Same pattern as [[Interview 005 — Real Estate Agency (Italy)]].

### On succession (critical market-level data)
- **"Only 20% manage to transfer."** Luigi estimates that among his long-standing artisan clients, roughly 80% fail to successfully hand over. This is a field-level data point from someone who sees the supply side of hundreds of SMEs daily. First quantitative succession-rate estimate from the field.
- **Two nieces entering — outcome uncertain.** One in administration (1 year), one in warehouse/production (1.5 months). Neither has strong business education ("livello medio"). Luigi explicitly questions whether they'll want to stay.
- **His own daughter chose a different path.** She went into furniture design. Not a rejection of business — but not a continuation of THIS business.
- **The drive matters more than the knowledge.** "Ci vogliono figure con la voglia di portare avanti" — you need people who WANT to carry it forward. Skill can be learned; motivation cannot be taught.

### On onboarding and learning
- **Two years minimum for any new employee.** Confirmed independently — same as the real estate agency (3+ years) and Vetreria Rachello (6 years for full autonomy). The pattern holds across sectors.
- **The learning is relational, not procedural.** Knowing that a specific obscure article moves 3 times a year but is critical for maintenance — this isn't in any manual. It's accumulated through daily exposure.
- **Even the gestionale takes time.** Despite being "simple," the system needs to be understood in context of 65,000 articles and decades of customer relationships.

### On technology and AI
- **Already using AI for writing and research.** Surprisingly progressive — uses AI for drafting letters to clients, product research via Google AI. His sister (born 1975) and his daughter (25) both use AI tools.
- **Doesn't know how to bring AI into the business systematically.** "Non so come approcciarmi" — the personal use is there, the business application is missing. Same "under-advised" pattern as [[Interview 006 - John Lynch (Lynka)]].
- **Resists changing the gestionale.** Knows other businesses have suffered months of crisis during gestionale changes. Prefers to keep "Easy" and modify it incrementally. Risk-averse on core systems.

### On the market
- **Young artisans are disappearing from his customer base.** Client age range is 40-70. Younger people buy online or use tutorials. The artisan customer base itself is aging — meaning the succession problem extends to his customers, not just his own business.
- **Italian creativity creates inventory complexity.** Italian manufacturers each build unique products, creating maintenance needs that last decades. This means knowledge of old products, obsolete parts, and historical inventory is irreplaceable — and it all lives in people's heads.

## Patterns Confirmed and New

### Confirmed
- **[[Patterns From Fieldwork]] Pattern 2 — Knowledge trapped at two levels** — Luigi holds the customer/product relationship knowledge. Employees hold operational/inventory knowledge. Neither layer is documented.
- **[[Patterns From Fieldwork]] Pattern 10 — The gestionale/ERP ceiling** — "Easy" handles transactions. Decision-making and advice live outside it entirely. "I processi interni ce li diamo noi."
- **[[Patterns From Fieldwork]] Pattern 11 — "Under-advised" as the felt problem** — Uses AI personally but doesn't know how to bring it into the business. No one is helping companies his size.
- **[[Patterns From Fieldwork]] Pattern 16 — Successor pool shrinks by choice** — Own daughter chose different sector. Nieces entering but "vedremo se hanno voglia di restare."
- **Long onboarding (2+ years)** — independently confirmed across a third sector (hardware retail after glass manufacturing and real estate).

### New

**Pattern 19 — The 80% succession failure rate (field estimate)**
From a hardware store owner who sees hundreds of artisan SMEs through his client base: "forse il 20% riesce a trasferire a qualcuno." Roughly 80% of 50-year-old businesses fail to transfer. This is the first field-level quantitative estimate and, if it holds, a powerful number for the pitch deck.
- **Source:** [[Interview 009 — Colusso Hardware (Italy)]]
- **Strength:** Moderate — single source, but informed by decades of observing hundreds of client businesses. Cross-reference with macro data needed.

**Pattern 20 — The advice-not-products shift**
SMEs that historically sold physical goods are increasingly selling knowledge/advice as their primary value proposition. "Stiamo sempre più offrendo il consiglio." The tacit knowledge IS the product now. This makes knowledge capture even more critical — you're not just documenting processes, you're preserving the core value proposition of the business itself.
- **Source:** [[Interview 009 — Colusso Hardware (Italy)]]
- **Strength:** Single source but directionally significant — likely applicable across many traditional retail/wholesale businesses
