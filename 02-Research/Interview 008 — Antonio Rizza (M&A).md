## What We Learned

### On operational due diligence as a deal-breaker
- Legal and tax issues can be protected through purchase contract clauses ("if a fine pops up from the tax authority, you protect us for 2-3 years")
- Operational issues CANNOT be contractually protected — if operations don't check out, the deal dies
- He uses a checklist of roughly 100 items for operational due diligence
- Scoring: 20/100 answered = no offer. 50/100 = conservative offer. 100/100 = willing to pay premium
- This means our product directly moves a company from "no offer" to "premium price" by improving documentation completeness

### On how documentation affects price
- Acquirers always factor in risk when calculating purchase price
- Less documentation = less visibility = higher perceived risk = larger discount on price
- More documentation = more visibility = lower risk = higher willingness to pay
- This is the most concrete "value created by our product" we've found — directly measurable as the difference between a 20% discount and a full-price offer

### On current solutions for knowledge transfer in M&A
- **Consulting agreements with founders:** 1-5 years post-acquisition, founder stays on to transfer knowledge. Can be combined with earn-out (bonus payments tied to revenue/profitability targets)
- **Retention programs for key employees:** One-off compensation tied to staying for a defined period
- **Shadowing:** Put someone trusted next to the founder for 3-5 years to learn "little by little"
- All of these are expensive, slow, and fragile. Our product could compress or replace the consulting/shadowing period

### On the acquirer's process
- **Make vs buy analysis:** Acquirers ask "how much would it cost to build this ourselves?" — that threshold determines maximum purchase price
- **Synergy assumptions:** Every acquisition justified by (1) cost synergies and (2) revenue synergies
- **Competitive bidding:** Typical process gives bidders 6 weeks for due diligence — better documentation = lower risk = stronger offer
- **Key departments involved:** Tech, HR, commercial, operations all validate before a deal proceeds

### On product validation
- Direct positive signal: "that would be very interesting" on the concept of pre-packaged operational documentation
- Suggested the product could evolve into a **sell-side advisory model**: do the documentation AND broker the deal
- Named the **AI-enabled virtual data room** as a product gap he has never seen filled
- Suggested **M&A freelancer marketplace** and **succession-specialised headhunting** as adjacent opportunities

### On barriers and risks
- **Trust is the #1 barrier:** Founders will worry about sharing company secrets, fear of data being used to compete against them
- **Monetisation unclear:** He pushed on "how do you make money?" — suggesting upfront fee (€5-10K range), success fee on eventual sale, or bundling into monthly fees
- **Credibility challenge:** Wolf's family business M&A experience (Translink) is the strongest credibility asset

### On pricing for sell-side advisory (market reference)
- Retainer: €20,000 to €100,000+ (one-off or monthly)
- Process duration: typically 6 months
- Success fee: 2-3% of purchase price
- For a €30-50M company, total advisory fees easily in the hundreds of thousands

**Pattern 17 — Documentation completeness directly determines deal price**
Acquirers use a ~100-item checklist. Documentation completeness maps linearly to price: 20% complete = no offer, 50% = conservative discount, 100% = premium price. Our product's M&A value proposition is not "nice to have" — it is the difference between getting an offer and not getting one.

**Pattern 18 — The consulting-agreement bridge is the current (bad) solution**
Post-acquisition knowledge transfer is currently handled through 1-5 year consulting agreements with founders + retention bonuses for key employees. Expensive, slow, creates governance tension, breaks down if the founder doesn't cooperate. Our product could compress this timeline dramatically.

## Strategic Implications

### The M&A angle is real and potentially the stronger GTM
- Selling to an SME owner: "pay €5-10K to document your operations" — hard sell
- Selling to an acquirer/PE fund: "we pre-package operational due diligence for target companies" — they already pay €100K+ for advisory, they understand the value immediately
- Antonio explicitly validated: "developing a network of businesses like that is amazingly valuable"

### The sell-side advisory model
- Antonio suggested: do the documentation AND broker the deal
- Revenue model: retainer + success fee (2-3% of deal value)
- Much larger revenue per client than the €5-10K diagnostic alone

### Wolf's credibility asset
- Antonio's strongest advice: "I've done this with my own company, and then I managed to sell it — now I'm replicating it for others"
- The Translink/M&A experience from the family business is unique and should be front-and-centre in all positioning
