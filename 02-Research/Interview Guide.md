# Interview Guide — SME Owners

#research #method #core #mom-test

> **Replaces:** Previous generic interview guide **Philosophy:** Mom Test — unbiased extraction, no leading questions, no loaded framing **Goal:** The interviewee realizes the fragility of their situation from their own position, without us telling them **Inspired by:** YC clip on the macro trend of knowledge loss in SMEs

---

## The Principle

We are not here to convince anyone of a problem. We are here to surface it.

The best outcome of an interview is the CEO saying something they've never said out loud before — and hearing themselves say it. Every question below is designed to create that moment. We never tell them they have a problem. We ask the question that makes them tell us.

---

## Phase 1 — Preparation Questions (sent before the meeting)

These go via email 2–3 days before the interview. Their purpose is to warm the CEO into the subject, set the stage, and make them feel this is a conversation about THEM, not about us selling something.

Send alongside the [[One-Pager]].

### Q1 — Operational focus

> What are the top 2–3 immediate operational challenges you are focusing on right now?

_Why this works:_ Gets them thinking in concrete terms. Whatever they name, we can connect to knowledge dependency in the live conversation.

### Q2 — Future concerns

> Looking at the next 5–10 years, what are your main concerns regarding securing the future and stability of the business?

_Why this works:_ Opens the succession/continuity door without us using those words. If they mention people, retirement, or key-person risk unprompted — that's a strong signal.

### Q3 — Succession status

> What considerations or preliminary steps have you taken regarding eventual succession or stepping back from daily operations?

_Why this works:_ Direct but not threatening. "Considerations or preliminary steps" gives them room to say "not much" without feeling judged.

### Q4 — Vision

> If everything goes perfectly over the next four years, what does a successful transition and a beautiful vision for your future look like?

_Why this works:_ Emotional, aspirational. Gets them imagining a positive outcome. Whatever they describe, we can later connect to "and what would need to be true for that to happen?"

## Phase 2 — Live Questions (asked only when triggered)

These are NEVER sent in advance. They only work live because they need to land in the right emotional moment. Each question has a specific trigger — a signal in the conversation that tells you it's time.

**Rule: Do not ask these cold. Wait for the trigger. If the trigger never comes, the question doesn't get asked.**

---

### 🔴 The Emotional Question

> **"What feelings does succession actually evoke for you?"**

**Trigger:** The founder gives a rehearsed, robotic answer about their five-year exit timeline. OR their body language shifts — they sigh, look tired, or seem stressed when talking about letting go.

**What you're listening for:** The gap between what they SAY (rational plan) and what they FEEL (fear, loss, identity). That gap is the real problem.

**Mom Test note:** This is the only question that directly names the emotional territory. Use it sparingly. If the founder is already being raw and honest, you don't need this — they're already there.

---

### 🟡 The Delegation Question

> **"Are you delegating this responsibility? If so, to whom?"**

**Trigger:** The founder talks about the overwhelming amount of work required to prepare for a sale. OR they mention a son, daughter, or junior partner who's supposed to eventually take over but "isn't quite ready yet."

**What you're listening for:** The name of the successor (or absence of one). The phrase "not ready yet" — what specifically isn't ready? The handwave that means "I haven't actually started delegating."

**Connects to:** [[Interview 001 — Successor]] — the three-body problem (awareness, authority, bandwidth in different hands). [[Four Sub-Problems]] — Problem 3 (emotional transition).

---

### 🔵 The Tech Friction Question

> **"How happy are you with the current degree of automation? When you've tried to implement new software recently — what was difficult? Why did it fail? Did it fail because the software couldn't handle your team's unwritten rules?"**

**Trigger:** The founder brings up a previous expensive IT project. Especially an ERP integration, a CRM rollout, or any system that was supposed to "fix everything."

**What you're listening for:** The phrase "it didn't fit how we work." The distinction between what the software captures (transactions, workflows) and what it doesn't (judgment, relationships, unwritten rules). The ERP ceiling — see [[Interview 006 - John Lynch (Lynka)]].

**Connects to:** [[Patterns From Fieldwork]] Pattern 10 — The ERP ceiling. [[Interview 002 - Ex-Banker, BoD of Deutsche Bank, SMEs view]] — Celonis/process mining tools that don't scale to SMEs.

---

### ⚫ The Bus Test (the trap)

> **"If your top two veteran employees were unreachable for a month, which specific daily operations would completely grind to a halt?"**

**Trigger:** The founder complains about a specific employee being overworked. They say something like "Klaus handles all of our logistics." They mention how hard it is for them or their senior staff to take a proper unplugged vacation.

**What you're listening for:** Specific names and specific functions. Every name they give you is a single point of failure. Every function they name is trapped knowledge. If they pause and think hard — they're realizing the fragility in real time. That pause is the most valuable data in the interview.

**⚠️ This is the most important question in the set.** It forces the founder to confront operational fragility without us claiming it exists. The answer is always uncomfortable. That discomfort is the signal.

**Connects to:** [[Problem Statement]] — "experts retire faster than they can be replaced." [[Patterns From Fieldwork]] Pattern 2 — knowledge trapped at two levels. [[Patterns From Fieldwork]] Pattern 5 — the irreplaceability myth.

---

### 🟢 The Onboarding Question

> **"If you hired a brilliant new manager tomorrow, how long would it take for them to become fully independent without shadowing you?"**

**Trigger:** The founder complains about the younger generation ("nobody wants to work"), the lack of skilled labor, or how hard it is to hire good middle management.

**What you're listening for:** The time estimate. If they say "6 months" — probe: what takes 6 months to learn? If they say "years" — that's your entire thesis confirmed in one word. If they say "they'd never fully replace me" — you've hit the irreplaceability myth live. Don't challenge it. Just note it.

**Connects to:** [[Patterns From Fieldwork]] Pattern 5 — the irreplaceability myth. [[Upstream vs Downstream]] — if onboarding takes years, the upstream intervention (starting early) is the only viable path.

---

### 🟣 The Fragmentation Question

> **"If I wanted to understand how your team handles a non-standard client request, how many different places — emails, notebooks, heads — would I have to look?"**

**Trigger:** The founder boasts about their flexibility or customized service. OR they complain about a recent mistake, miscommunication between departments, or an order that took too long.

**What you're listening for:** The word "heads." The moment they say "well, you'd have to ask Maria, and then check with..." — they're mapping their own fragility for you. If the answer is "it's all in here" (pointing to their own head) — that's the problem stated in one gesture.

**Connects to:** [[Problem Statement]] — "tacit knowledge can be observed, mapped, and turned into something the rest of the company can use." [[Interview 004 - Prisma Founder (Digital Agency, Romania)]] Pattern 9 — the neutral mirror (we document what they already know).

## Framework Notes

### The Mom Test Alignment

- **Never ask:** "Would you use a product that documents your expertise?" (hypothetical, invites polite lies)
- **Never ask:** "Do you think knowledge loss is a problem?" (leading, they'll agree to be nice)
- **Always ask:** Questions about their past behavior, specific situations, concrete examples
- **The goal:** Facts about their life, not opinions about our idea

### Reading the Room

- If the founder is guarded → stay in Phase 1 territory, don't push live questions
- If the founder is open and storytelling → let them talk, use live questions only to redirect
- If the founder gets emotional → do NOT fill the silence. Let them sit with what they just said.
- If the founder gets defensive → back off, return to operational questions, try again later

### What Success Looks Like

The interview worked if:

1. The founder said something they've never articulated before
2. We have 2+ specific names of people who are single points of failure
3. We have a concrete time estimate for how long it would take to replace the founder's judgment
4. The founder asks US a question back — "so what are you actually building?" (means we earned their curiosity without pitching)

## Version History

### v0 — Original Questions (pre-Mom Test)

The first set of questions sent to SMEs before the current methodology was developed. Broad and partially leading — replaced once the Mom Test principle was applied.

> A glimpse into your vision of an ideal future
>
> 1. What are your top 3 issues that need to be solved right now?
> 2. Presently (including 2025), what are the 3 main overarching pains in running your business?
> 3. What are your main concerns regarding securing your business future (next 5-10 years)?
>    - How happy are you with your company's degree of: Digitalisation / Automation / AI integration?
>    - What's your biggest hurdle to reaching satisfaction in this respect?
> 4. What considerations have you had regarding succession?
>    - What feelings does succession evoke?
>    - Are you delegating this responsibility? If so, to who?
>
> Give us a glimpse of what a beautiful vision looks like to you regarding your future.

**Why replaced:** "What feelings does succession evoke?" asked cold is leading — it names the emotional territory before the founder does. "Top 3 issues" is too generic to surface knowledge-dependency pain. The vision framing invited aspirational answers rather than concrete stories about past behavior.
