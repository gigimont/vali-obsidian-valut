## Context

Lynka is on its **fourth ERP system**, a Microsoft Business Central rollout that took 30 months and cost over €1M. They are operationally sophisticated, mid-implementation pain, and explicitly preparing for an exit in 3+ years. John was an early AI learner three years ago and now feels his company has fallen behind. He is engaged in conversations with a competitor of ours (Omnivisor, Warsaw-based AI consultancy) and has used multiple operations consultants over the years.

This is not the founder running on intuition that our one-pager describes. This is a **digitally mature, exit-preparing CEO** who can tell us exactly where our thesis breaks and where it holds.

## What We Learned

### On the ERP capturing tacit knowledge

We assumed ERPs capture only transactions and miss the tacit decision layer. **John challenged this directly.** Lynka's ERP captures 30–40 processes end-to-end, including non-standard client requests (which get channeled through the system via new product cards). Even email communication is being replaced by ERP workflow. **Anyone in the order-processing chain has their work substantially represented in the system.**

The exceptions are: marketing, HR, graphic design, IT, and partial sales — roles where the work is creative, judgment-based, or external-facing. Production workers register output in ERP but do most work on machines.

**Implication:** Our "ERP doesn't capture tacit knowledge" framing is true, but the gap is narrower than our one-pager implies. For a digitally mature SME, the untouched layer is the **judgment, creative, and human-relations work** — not the transactional flow.

### On consultants and implementation

This was the most painful and instructive part of the conversation. John has hired:

1. An **industry-expert operations consultant** — biweekly visits for 2–3 years. Excellent at coaching John. **Cannot get changes implemented through John's team.**
2. **Cost-savings consultants** with 25%-of-savings success fees who found real savings on cardboard boxes (€500K/year). **Lynka never used any of their recommendations.**
3. **Omnivisor** (Warsaw-based AI consultancy, founder Radek Miszkont) — proposed concrete AI projects to reduce headcount and improve processes. Lynka hasn't pulled the trigger yet.

**Pattern:** Insight without implementation is worth nothing. Consultants who deliver only diagnosis get sidelined. **John's quote on us:** *"You need to get a couple beta clients where you actually have a success and then you have people who can testify."*

### On change management

Even John, the owner, cannot reliably push changes through his organization. His operations consultant fails at it. The boss "has no impact" on stubbornness. The only options are firing or routing around resistant employees.

**Implication:** Our deliverable cannot be a blueprint or document. It must be — or be paired with — an **implementation mechanism.** This is a major finding and likely a strategic pivot.

### On succession and exit

John is preparing for a sale in 3+ years. He hired a CFO as step one but has no operational successor. Either he sells to a buyer who installs their own person, or he needs to develop someone. The succession problem is real and unresolved.

When asked what a buyer would see "under the hood beyond financials," John said the operational excellence of the company would be visible, but the decision-making knowledge lives with him and would require a 1-year transition period where he stays on to train the buyer's incoming person.

**Implication:** Our M&A-readiness positioning is validated. The "decision-making layer not captured anywhere" is a real exposure even at this digital maturity level.

### On labor scarcity

Krakow unemployment is 2% — below natural levels. Wages rising, hard to find people. Production moving to Poland from Western Europe makes it worse. This validates the **knowledge protection** angle: when you can't easily replace people, the knowledge in their heads becomes more strategically valuable.

> "Either we have to raise our wages, or we go without people. And there's not always technology to replace people."

### On onboarding speed

When asked if reducing time-to-independence for new hires would be valuable, John said:

> "Even McDonald's, if they can get people up in two weeks instead of one week, it's 400% improvement. It's a no-brainer."

Onboarding currently takes 6–12 months in his business for someone to become net-positive. **This is one of the strongest validations of the day** — and a potential angle for the deliverable.

### On the AI gap

John was an early AI learner three years ago, attended conferences, was at the cutting edge. Today his company uses AI only as a translator and copywriter. Process-improvement AI? "Almost zero. Zilch."

> "I know what we need to do. Pick a little process, find a solution in AI, make it happen, and start making improvements."

**He knows what to do but isn't doing it.** This is the gap our product could fill — not by teaching AI strategy (he already knows), but by **executing** the small process improvements he doesn't have bandwidth for.

### On the realistic value-delivery timeline

We pitched "value within two to three weeks." John explicitly rejected this:

- 2 weeks: unrealistic — you haven't even met the key people
- 3 months: hint of progress, promise of something working
- 6 months: confirmed, felt value-add

**He also told us pricing reality:** "Fairly humble amounts of money in the beginning. So companies can afford that. But they want to feel after three months that they're actually getting some bang for their buck."

## Upstream or downstream?

**John is downstream.** He is too far along the digitalization and exit-preparation journey for our offering to fit cleanly. He has the ERP. He has the CFO. He has the exit timeline. He's evaluating direct competitors (Omnivisor).

This is a strong data point for the [[Upstream vs Downstream]] question: our wedge should serve founders **earlier** — before the ERP, before the CFO hire, before the buyer conversations. By the time someone looks like John, multiple players are competing for the same wallet and the gap is narrower.

**John's value to us is as a positive outlier and validator, not as a customer.** Like the [[Interview 005 — Real Estate Agency (Italy)]] case — these are the companies that have *partially solved* the problem, and their solutions tell us what real value looks like.

## New Patterns to Add to [[Patterns From Fieldwork]]

10. **The ERP ceiling.** Even sophisticated ERPs (30+ processes captured) leave the decision-making, judgment, and relational layers untouched. The gap is real but narrower than our one-pager implies. The untouched roles are marketing, HR, creative, IT, and parts of sales.

11. **Insight without implementation gets sidelined.** Consultants who deliver only diagnosis — even excellent ones — fail to drive change. Cost-savings consultants identified real savings (€500K/year on packaging) and Lynka never adopted them. Operations consultants coach but cannot make change happen through the team. **Our deliverable must include an implementation mechanism, not just a blueprint.**

12. **Realistic value-delivery horizon: 3–6 months, not weeks.** Owners who have hired consultants before know that meaningful change takes time. They expect "hint of progress" at 3 months and "confirmed value-add" at 6 months. Our two-to-three-week framing is naïve and damages credibility.

13. **The "under-advised on AI" feeling is universal — but differently expressed.** Early adopters who fell behind feel it as "we used to be cutting edge, now we're behind." Founders who never started feel it as "the big players ignore us." Same gap, different emotional charge.

14. **Labor scarcity makes knowledge protection more valuable.** Sub-2% unemployment in operating regions means key people cannot be replaced quickly or cheaply. When the labor market is tight, the knowledge in employees' heads becomes a higher-stakes asset.

15. **Onboarding speed as a measurable, universally-valued metric.** Every owner intuitively understands that reducing time-to-independence for new hires is enormous value. This is a more concrete and resonant framing than "documenting tacit knowledge."

## Surprises

1. **John pushed back hard on the timeline.** We expected resistance to AI hype but not to the value-delivery promise. The two-to-three-week framing is not just unrealistic — it actively signals naïveté. Drop it.

2. **The ERP captures more than we thought.** Our one-pager's "founder runs on intuition" archetype assumes a much earlier-stage company than Lynka. Need to be precise about ICP digitalization stage.

3. **Direct competitor surfaced unprompted.** Omnivisor is operating in our space, in adjacent geography, with similar positioning, and is already engaging with John. Need to research them seriously and quickly.

4. **The 25%-success-fee model has been tried and failed in this market.** The cost-savings consultants found real savings and were ignored. Adoption is the bottleneck, not insight.

5. **John's framing of his ideal product:** "Pick a little process, find a solution in AI, make it happen." This is much narrower and more concrete than our blueprint vision. It's almost an "AI implementation as a service" model.

6. **Owner has no leverage on stubbornness.** Even John, the boss, cannot get his team to adopt changes. This challenges our implicit assumption that founder buy-in equals organizational buy-in.
