# Interview 007 — Vetreria Rachello (Italy)

#research #interview #sme-owner #manufacturing #italy #succession-crisis

> **Date:** May 2026
> **Interviewee:** Marco Rachello — owner of a glass manufacturing company (vetreria)
> **Sector:** Glass cutting and insulated glass unit manufacturing (vetrocamere)
> **Geography:** Veneto, Italy
> **Business age:** ~47 years (founded 1979 by father, Marco joined 1986)
> **Current team:** 4 people (Marco, brother-in-law Luca, two women employees)
> **Previous team:** 8 people (downsized in January 2025)
> **Revenue:** ~€1M current (down from €2M peak in 2023-24)
> **Clients:** ~80 active, ranging from small carpenters to large manufacturers (Agostini Group, 250 employees)
> **Format:** In-person conversation in Italian (transcript available)
> **Succession status:** Retiring in ~3 years. No confirmed successor. Almost sold in 2024 — family blocked it.

---

## ⚠️ Significance of This Interview

**This is the most complete validation of the thesis to date.** Marco has LIVED through a catastrophic overnight succession (father died 2007, he took over instantly at 35) AND is now about to create the exact same scenario himself — retiring in 3 years with no successor, all knowledge in his head, a potential buyer who was scared off by family emotion. He is simultaneously the evidence of the problem and the person about to reproduce it.

This is also the first interview where the interviewee explicitly named the decision-making gap as the barrier to succession — through the lens of Lisa, his 19-year employee who could take over but is "afraid of making decisions."

---

## The Story

### Origin (1979–2007)
- Father started the glass business in 1979, working from a rented space
- Marco joined at 13.5 years old in 1986
- Family business: father, uncle (later left), two sisters, then Marco + sister Barbara
- In 2000, uncle left due to conflicts — Marco took over his role alongside father
- Business grew organically through reputation and relationships — minimal advertising ever needed

### The Catastrophic Succession (2007)
- Father died suddenly in May 2007
- Marco, age 35, took over "dalla sera alla mattina" (overnight)
- First six months: "ogni sera piangevo" (cried every evening)
- On DAY THREE after his father's death, the director of Agostini Group (major client, 250 employees) called and said: "Are you continuing or closing? Decide and let me know."
- Some clients were compassionate. Others: "il terzo giorno ne frega un cazzo" (by day three, they don't give a damn)
- It took 6 years before he felt stable

### The Business Today
- Downsized from 8 to 4 employees in January 2025
- Deliberately dropped two largest clients (Agostini Group after 29 years, Futura Serramenti after 22 years)
- Revenue halved from ~€2M to ~€1M
- No debt, owns the property and equipment outright
- Still highly valued by remaining clients — some refused to let him quit, came to his office and said "you MUST keep supplying us"

### The Failed Sale (2024)
- In July 2024, a window manufacturer (Futura Serramenti) offered to buy the business
- Deal: €550K for the operations, client portfolio, and staff (Marco would keep the two buildings and rent them out)
- Marco would stay on at €3K/month net salary
- Both parties agreed. Went to lawyers and accountants.
- In September, Marco's brother-in-law and mother blocked it: "era del mio papà" (it was your father's)
- Marco was devastated for 10 days, then accepted and moved on
- He knows the value is declining: "fra tre anni se l'azienda non va avanti, ti comprano per niente" (in three years if the business doesn't continue, they'll buy it for nothing)

### The Succession Void
- Three children, none willing to take over
- Daughter Greta (24): "papà con te tutto il giorno anche no" (dad, with you all day, no thanks)
- Son Nicola: possible but unlikely, works elsewhere
- Youngest son Andrea (14): a pianist, not business-oriented
- Lisa (19-year employee): only realistic internal successor
  - Marco has offered to hand over the business to her
  - She says she doesn't have the courage or character
  - Marco believes she could do it: "secondo me se insisto, qualcosa sì"
  - Her specific fear: making decisions, confronting clients, pricing

## What We Learned

### On knowledge concentration (STRONGEST SIGNAL YET)
- ALL pricing knowledge is in Marco's head — his business partner of 25+ years doesn't know prices
- Client relationships are personal: carpenters want to see Marco, not anyone else
- Same pattern existed with his father: clients wanted the father, not Marco, until the father died
- Competitive advantage is in execution quality that Marco can feel but hasn't documented: a client said "I can install 50 of your glass units in the time it takes to install 25 from anyone else"
- Pricing decisions require real-time judgment — he hasn't been able to maintain a price list longer than 1-1.5 months due to volatile costs

### On the decision-making gap (THESIS VALIDATION)
- Lisa, the most realistic successor, has been in the business 19 years
- She can do the operational work
- What she CANNOT do: make pricing decisions, confront clients, decide to take or refuse a job
- This is not a training problem — it is a confidence-in-judgment problem
- Marco's assessment: she could learn if pushed, but "the first times she'd make mistakes"
- This maps perfectly to the "decision replication" concept from [[Interview 003 - Merih (Finance Professor)]] / Clonable

### On emotional barriers to succession
- The failed sale was blocked by family emotion, not business logic
- Marco himself couldn't sleep: "pensavo mio papà dentro in bara si gira" (I thought my father in his coffin would turn over)
- His children refuse to enter the business because they've witnessed the psychological toll
- Lisa's fear isn't about competence — it's about the emotional weight of being the decision-maker
- The pattern repeats: Marco's father's clients didn't trust Marco. Marco's clients won't trust Lisa. Trust transfers through years of proximity, not through documentation alone.

### On business value erosion in real-time
- He's actively dismantling the business: removed advertising, removed Google presence, removed road signs
- Revenue halved in one year (€2M → €1M) by deliberate choice
- The €550K offer from 2024 would be lower today and will be lower still in 3 years
- He explicitly said: "in three years they'll buy it for nothing"
- Every month without a succession plan, enterprise value leaks

### On the human cost
- 40 years of work, starting at 13.5 years old
- Saturdays, Sundays, nights
- Father's death: 6 years to stabilise
- Employee management: "the last years after covid, people have become much, much worse"
- Fired 4 of 6 employees in one decision — had to pay all TFR (severance) within 6 months while absorbing a €36K bad debt
- Lost sleep over the failed sale
- "Il fegato si è liberato" (my liver is freed) — now that he's reduced the load, he feels physically better

## Patterns Confirmed, Challenged, and New

### Confirmed (strongest confirmation yet)
- **[[Patterns From Fieldwork]] Pattern 2 — Knowledge trapped at two levels** — Marco holds all pricing/client knowledge. Even his 25-year business partner doesn't know prices.
- **[[Patterns From Fieldwork]] Pattern 5 — Irreplaceability myth** — except here it's not a myth. The clients genuinely want HIM. When his father died, the same happened. It's structural, not ego.
- **[[Patterns From Fieldwork]] Pattern 1 — Profitability masks fragility** — the business was doing €2M/year and seemed fine. Underneath, all knowledge was in one head, no succession plan existed, and the whole thing was one health event away from collapse.

### New Patterns

**Pattern 13 — The decision-making gap as the specific succession barrier**
Lisa can do the work. She cannot make the judgment calls. This is not a knowledge gap (she's seen it done for 19 years) — it's a decision-confidence gap. The product implication: documenting processes is necessary but not sufficient. The real value is capturing HOW the founder decides (pricing, client management, risk assessment) and creating a system that supports decision-making for the successor. This directly validates the Clonable / "decision replication" direction from [[Interview 003 - Merih (Finance Professor)]].

**Pattern 14 — Family emotion as a deal-killer**
The sale was agreed, lawyered, priced. Family blocked it for purely emotional reasons. This is not irrational — it's the most human thing in the world. But it means: any succession solution that doesn't address the emotional dimension will fail even when the business logic is clear. The [[Council Verdict]] flagged this as Sub-Problem 3 (Emotional Transition). This interview proves it's not separable from the other sub-problems.

**Pattern 15 — Value erosion without succession**
The business is losing value every month that Marco doesn't have a plan. €550K offer in 2024, probably less today, "nothing" in 3 years by his own estimate. This is a quantifiable cost of inaction — and it's the kind of number that could go in the pitch deck. The problem isn't just "knowledge loss" in the abstract. It's measurable enterprise value destruction.

**Pattern 16 — The successor pool is shrinking by choice, not by demographics**
His children CAN work. They choose not to because they've seen the life. Lisa CAN take over. She chooses not to because she's afraid of the decisions. The bottleneck isn't finding people — it's making the role survivable and the transition supportable.

## Strategic Implications

### For the product
- Documentation alone won't solve Lisa's problem. She needs decision-support: "when this situation arises, here's how Marco would decide and why."
- The product might need TWO outputs: (1) operational documentation (processes, clients, suppliers) and (2) decision-replication system (how the founder prices, negotiates, assesses risk)
- Output (2) is what makes Lisa confident enough to take over. Output (1) alone leaves her with the same fear.

### For positioning and sales
- The value-erosion angle is powerful and quantifiable: "your business was worth €550K last year. Without a succession plan, it'll be worth nothing in 3 years. Our service costs €X."
- Marco would probably have been a buyer of this product BEFORE the failed sale — to make the business more transferable and increase the sale price
- The pitch to founders like Marco isn't "document your processes" — it's "protect the value of what you built"

### For the pitch deck
- Marco's story (anonymised) is a perfect case study slide: catastrophic overnight succession in 2007, now facing the same cliff, business value eroding
- The €550K → €0 in 3 years arc is the most concrete "cost of inaction" number we've found
