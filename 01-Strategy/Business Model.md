## Framing: What We Are Selling

Before choosing a revenue model, the framing question: do we pitch this as a **service**, a **SaaS platform**, or something else?

**The right framing: Tech-Enabled Productized Service**

- To the SME founder: a white-glove, done-for-you service. Fixed price. Fixed scope. Fixed timeline. They do nothing except give us access.
- To us internally: a scalable tech platform where AI does 90% of the extraction work, making each engagement more efficient over time.

**Why not "SaaS Platform":** A 60-year-old SME owner hears "SaaS" as another login, another subscription, another software to learn. Immediate rejection.
**Why not "consulting":** A founder hears "consulting" as expensive, disruptive, and someone telling them how to run their business. Immediate rejection.
**Why this works:** Productized service = fixed price, fixed scope, fixed timeline. No ambiguity. No hourly billing. They're buying an outcome, not our time.

## Revenue Model Options

### Option 1: Break-Even Report + Upsell Consulting (Wolf's initial model)

Inspired by energy efficiency companies that audit homes at cost and earn on product sales.

- **How it works:** Deliver the Organizational Report at or near break-even. Use the engagement to convert clients to higher-margin consulting, digitalization, and AI implementation services.
- **Strengths:** Dramatically lowers the entry barrier for skeptical founders. Builds massive trust. Gets us inside the business.
- **Weaknesses:** High upfront operational risk. Founders may take the report, use it to sell their business, and never buy the upsell. Conversion depends on relationship quality and timing.

### Option 2: M&A Broker Channel — B2B2B

Instead of selling to founders (who are resistant), sell to **M&A advisors and business brokers**.

- **How it works:** Brokers lose money when deals collapse due to messy, undocumented operations. We charge brokers a fee to "prep" their client's operations before due diligence opens.
- **Strengths:** The broker has a direct financial incentive to buy (deal protection). Brokers have a ready pipeline of clients. We avoid the hardest sales conversation — convincing a founder he has a problem.
- **Weaknesses:** Dependent on broker adoption. Broker may commoditize our service. We lose the direct founder relationship, which is where future upsell lives.

### Option 3: Due Diligence Premium

A high-ticket, one-off fee (e.g., €10,000–20,000) paid when a company actively enters the M&A preparation phase.

- **How it works:** Positioned as "operational due diligence" — the slice of due diligence that traditionally falls through the cracks. Justified by the report preventing a buyer from slashing valuation by hundreds of thousands of euros.
- **Strengths:** Clear ROI justification. One-time payment, no recurring commitment anxiety. High margin if AI handles the heavy lifting.
- **Weaknesses:** Sales cycle tied to succession/sale timelines, which are unpredictable. Requires the founder to be actively "ready" — not suitable for the upstream market.

### Option 4: Subscription — "Living Documentation"

Monthly fee (e.g., €500/month) for a platform that continuously updates the organizational report as the company evolves.

- **How it works:** Report isn't static — it refreshes as people, processes, and systems change.
- **Strengths:** Recurring revenue. Valuable for new buyers/successors who want ongoing, up-to-date documentation.
- **Weaknesses:** Traditional founders reject recurring software fees for tools they rarely look at daily. Better fit for Phase 3 (when younger, digital-native management takes over post-sale). Not an MVP candidate.

## Three-Phase Scaling Model

### Phase 1 — Manual Service (Do Things That Don't Scale)

Heavy human involvement. AI tools assist but humans review and fill gaps. Goal: learn what the AI needs, where it fails, and what a quality output looks like. Deploy on known businesses first (team networks, family connections).

### Phase 2 — Platform Automation

As models train on the messy realities of SME engagements, human time per report drops significantly. Margins expand. The service becomes more efficient per engagement without a proportional increase in cost.

### Phase 3 — The Ecosystem Upsell

Once the business is documented (and often sold or transitioned), the new owner/successor wants efficiency, not just transparency. Because we already mapped the operations, we become the natural vendor for:
- Workflow automation
- IT infrastructure modernization
- Digitalization projects
- AI tool implementation and training

The report is the foot in the door. Phase 3 is where real margin lives.

## GTM Strategy

### Channel Priority

1. **Known networks first** — team connections and family businesses as zero-acquisition-cost sandboxes, generating first case studies (see [[Origin Story]])
2. **M&A brokers and intermediaries** — commercialisti in Italy (see [[Outreach Strategy]]), business brokers in Germany. Sell through trusted advisors who have access to founders we can't reach directly.
3. **Direct founder outreach** — lowest yield, but necessary for market education and for the upstream (non-M&A) customer

### Pricing Anchor

First validated signal from [[Interview 004 - Prisma Founder (Digital Agency, Romania)]]: **€5,000–10,000** for the diagnostic report was mentioned without resistance, positioned against consulting boutiques that charge far more over longer timelines.
